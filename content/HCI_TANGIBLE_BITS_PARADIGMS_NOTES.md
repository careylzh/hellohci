## Notes By Concept

### Tangible Bits

Source: Hiroshi Ishii and Brygg Ullmer, "Tangible Bits: Towards Seamless Interfaces between People, Bits and Atoms," CHI 1997. MIT Media Lab and ACM CHI records.

- The paradigm argues that GUIs trap digital information as "painted bits" on screens, while everyday life already has rich physical skills, spatial memory, tactile feedback, and peripheral awareness.
- It proposes three linked design moves: interactive architectural surfaces, coupling digital information to graspable physical objects, and ambient media for background awareness.
- The key shift is from interacting with representations of things to manipulating things that are themselves bound to computation.
- The prototypes, including metaDESK, transBOARD, and ambientROOM, show Tangible Bits as a research program rather than a single device style.
- Field-shaping lesson: Tangible Bits makes HCI a question of material, body, room, and attention, not just display, pointing device, and task.

Relation to Tangible Bits: This is the anchor concept. The remaining paradigms either supply its intellectual background, adjacent technical strategies, or later theoretical vocabulary.

### Ubiquitous Computing

Source: Mark Weiser, "The Computer for the 21st Century," Scientific American, 1991.

- Weiser's vision shifts the unit of computing from a single personal machine to many networked devices embedded in the environment.
- The famous design ambition is that the most profound technologies disappear into everyday life by becoming ordinary infrastructure.
- Tabs, pads, and boards illustrate scale-sensitive computational artifacts distributed through rooms and work practices.
- Ubiquitous computing makes attention a central design material: the system should support action without forcing itself into consciousness.
- It also raises early versions of today's privacy, infrastructure, and social adoption questions around pervasive sensing.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Tangible Bits inherits ubicomp's environmental ambition but makes the physical coupling stronger: not just many computers everywhere, but bits bound to surfaces, objects, and ambient materials.

### Calm Technology

Source: Mark Weiser and John Seely Brown, "Designing Calm Technology," 1995.

- Calm technology reframes interaction as attention management, especially movement between peripheral awareness and focused attention.
- The Dangling String example shows that computational state can be sensed through motion, sound, and peripheral perception without occupying a screen.
- The paradigm values legibility without interruption: a system should inform without demanding constant explicit interaction.
- Calmness is not passivity; it is the ability to become salient at the right moment and recede afterward.
- It gives HCI a vocabulary for background information displays, ambient cues, and low-friction awareness.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Calm technology directly informs Tangible Bits' ambient media strand: light, airflow, water, and sound can carry digital information at the edge of perception.

### Computer-Augmented Environments

Source: Pierre Wellner, "Interacting with Paper on the DigitalDesk," Communications of the ACM, 1993.

- DigitalDesk is a canonical early prototype of a computer-augmented environment: physical paper, projected digital interaction, cameras, and recognition are combined on a desk surface.
- It treats paper as a durable, high-resolution, socially familiar medium rather than something computation must replace.
- The work demonstrates hybrid interaction: users can write, point, copy, calculate, and manipulate digital overlays while keeping physical documents in play.
- It is an early model of surface computing and document-centered augmented reality.
- The deeper idea is continuity between legacy work practices and computation: innovation happens by augmenting material routines.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Computer-augmented environments are a direct ancestor of interactive surfaces in Tangible Bits, especially the idea that tables, desks, and paper can become computational interfaces.

### Graspable User Interfaces

Source: George Fitzmaurice, Hiroshi Ishii, and William Buxton, "Bricks: Laying the Foundations for Graspable User Interfaces," CHI 1995.

- Graspable interfaces use physical artifacts, or "bricks," as handles attached to virtual objects or functions.
- They challenge the mouse's time-multiplexed style by allowing space-multiplexed control: different physical objects can control different things at the same time.
- The approach supports two-handed input, spatial memory, parallel action, and tactile/kinesthetic feedback.
- It reframes input devices as representational and manipulable objects, not just generic control channels.
- The design space helps explain why physical form, placement, and coupling matter for interaction bandwidth.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Graspable UIs are one of Tangible Bits' core technical foundations: they supply the "coupling of bits and atoms" move.

### Augmented Reality

Source: Steven Feiner, Blair MacIntyre, and Doree Seligmann, "Knowledge-based Augmented Reality," Communications of the ACM, 1993.

- Augmented reality overlays digital information onto a user's perception of physical space.
- The 1993 knowledge-based framing emphasizes systems that decide what annotations or guidance are relevant in a given task context.
- AR makes the world itself the display surface, reducing the separation between physical action and digital instruction.
- It foregrounds registration, context, attention, and semantic fit between digital overlay and real-world object.
- Field-shaping lesson: AR expands HCI from operating computers to perceiving and acting through computationally annotated environments.

Relation to Tangible Bits: AR and Tangible Bits share the goal of bridging physical and digital worlds, but AR often overlays information visually while Tangible Bits emphasizes material manipulation and ambient physical media.

### Wearable Computing

Source: Steve Mann, "Smart Clothing: The Wearable Computer and WearCam," Personal Technologies, 1997; related ACM Multimedia 1996 smart clothing paper.

- Wearable computing makes the computer continuous, body-worn, hands-free, and available during ordinary activity.
- Mann's work emphasizes first-person sensing and display: the system can share the user's viewpoint rather than sit in the room as external infrastructure.
- Wearables shift HCI toward perception/action augmentation, memory, personal imaging, and mediated reality.
- The paradigm also introduces power relations: who controls sensing, visibility, and augmentation, the person or the environment?
- It foreshadows modern smart glasses, lifelogging, activity trackers, and body-centered AI assistants.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Wearables complement Tangible Bits by moving computation onto the body. Tangible Bits augments rooms and artifacts; wearable computing augments the user's ongoing embodied perspective.

### Media Spaces

Source: Sara Bly, Steve Harrison, and Susan Irwin, "Media Spaces: Bringing People Together in a Video, Audio, and Computing Environment," Communications of the ACM, 1993.

- Media spaces are persistent audio/video/computing environments that let distributed groups work together and maintain informal awareness.
- They treat collaboration as more than scheduled meetings: hallway talk, presence, overhearing, and opportunistic interaction matter.
- The technology creates a shared social place across distance, blending task work with background social connection.
- Media spaces expose privacy, control, and etiquette as core technical design questions.
- They shaped CSCW by showing that "being together" is an interactional resource, not merely a communication channel.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Media spaces influenced Tangible Bits' concern with background awareness and architectural interfaces, especially the idea that rooms can mediate social presence as well as task input.

### Context-Aware Computing

Source: Bill Schilit, Norman Adams, and Roy Want, "Context-Aware Computing Applications," IEEE Workshop on Mobile Computing Systems and Applications, 1994.

- Context-aware systems adapt to where they are used, who and what is nearby, available devices, and changes over time.
- The paradigm turns context from a human inference into a computational input.
- Early applications included location-sensitive information, nearby-resource discovery, and adaptive behavior in mobile environments.
- Its lasting challenge is modeling context without flattening messy social activity into brittle variables.
- It is foundational for mobile, pervasive, IoT, recommender, and smart-environment systems.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Tangible Bits needs context awareness to make physical-digital coupling meaningful: an object, room, or ambient display must know enough about location, activity, and social setting to respond appropriately.

### Embodied Interaction

Source: Paul Dourish, "Where the Action Is: The Foundations of Embodied Interaction," MIT Press, 2001.

- Dourish gives theoretical grounding to tangible and social computing through phenomenology and situated action.
- Embodied interaction means meaning emerges in practical engagement with the world, not only in internal plans or symbolic representations.
- The framework connects physical manipulation, social practice, and interpretive context.
- It critiques purely cognitive or information-processing views of HCI by emphasizing skilled, situated activity.
- It remains field-shaping because it explains why material form, bodily habit, social norms, and place are not secondary interface details.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Embodied interaction is the theory that helps explain why Tangible Bits matters: tangible systems work because people make sense through bodily, situated manipulation.

### Reactive Environments

Source: Jeremy Cooperstock, Sidney Fels, William Buxton, and Kenneth Smith, "Reactive Environments," Communications of the ACM, 1997; William Buxton, "Living in Augmented Reality: Ubiquitous Media and Reactive Environments," 1997.

- Reactive environments are rooms or architectural settings that sense human activity and respond through media, computation, or physical effects.
- The room becomes an active participant in interaction rather than a passive container for devices.
- Buxton's related "ubiquitous media" framing combines media spaces with ubicomp: communication, computation, and place become one design material.
- The paradigm foregrounds proximal sensing, context-sensitive interaction, and architectural-scale feedback.
- It anticipates smart rooms, instrumented studios, immersive installations, and responsive built environments.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Reactive environments overlap strongly with Tangible Bits' ambientROOM and interactive surfaces: both ask how walls, rooms, and architectural media can become computational interfaces.

### Slow Technology

Source: Lars Hallnas and Johan Redstrom, "Slow Technology - Designing for Reflection," Personal and Ubiquitous Computing, 2001.

- Slow technology resists the assumption that better interaction always means faster, more efficient task completion.
- It argues for systems that support reflection, mental rest, duration, ambiguity, and long-term relationship.
- Examples such as soniture and informative art position technology as something lived with rather than merely used.
- The paradigm matters because ubiquitous computing places technology in everyday life for long periods; therefore, its tempo and meaning matter.
- It gives designers a language for deliberate friction, aesthetic presence, and reflective awareness.

Relation to Tangible Bits: Slow technology extends Tangible Bits' ambient and material concerns toward temporality: physical-digital systems should sometimes invite reflection instead of immediate manipulation.

## Cross-Cutting Synthesis

The largest pattern is a move away from the desktop computer as HCI's default scene. These paradigms relocate interaction into objects, bodies, paper, rooms, architecture, remote social spaces, and everyday routines.

Tangible Bits sits at the intersection of four major currents:

- Environmental computing: ubicomp, reactive environments, media spaces, and context-aware computing.
- Material interaction: computer-augmented environments, graspable user interfaces, and tangible interfaces.
- Attention and temporality: calm technology and slow technology.
- Embodiment and perception: embodied interaction, augmented reality, and wearable computing.

The field-shaping contribution is not simply "make interfaces physical." It is the broader claim that computation becomes more powerful and humane when it is designed through material coupling, peripheral awareness, situated context, and embodied practice.
