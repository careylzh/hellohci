# HCI Theory Note: Counter-Movements, Technoaesthetics, and Slow Technology

## 1. Core framing

Mainstream HCI often optimises for:

* speed
* usability
* productivity
* automation
* engagement
* scalability
* frictionless interaction

Counter-movements ask:

> What if these “good” values also create harm, exhaustion, sameness, or loss of meaning?

They are like **off-meta picks in Mobile Legends**: strange at first, but powerful because they expose blind spots in the dominant meta.

---

## 2. Slow Technology

**Key authors:** Lars Hallnäs & Johan Redström
**Core idea:** Not all technology should be faster, smoother, or more efficient.

Slow Technology designs for:

* reflection
* presence
* long-term use
* ambiguity
* aesthetic experience
* mental rest

Simple version:

> Slow Technology pushes back against speed culture in design.

It does not mean “bad usability.”
It means technology can support a slower, richer relationship with time.

---

## 3. Technoaesthetics

Technoaesthetics asks:

> Why do some technologies feel joyful, elegant, satisfying, or meaningful to use?

It studies technology as an aesthetic experience, not just a functional tool.

Example: a MacBook keyboard can feel good because of:

* sound
* tactility
* timing
* visual feedback
* material quality
* bodily rhythm
* environmental context

So the “interface” is not only the screen.
It includes the body, senses, material, rhythm, and atmosphere.

## 5. Known HCI theory anchors

Useful anchors to revisit:

* **Slow Technology** — reflection over speed
* **Calm Technology** — peripheral, non-demanding interaction
* **Reflective Design** — making users think about assumptions and values
* **Feminist HCI** — care, agency, embodiment, pluralism, power
* **Sustainable HCI** — repair, limits, ecology, long-term consequences
* **More-than-human design** — designing with nonhuman life, materials, environments
* **Decolonial / pluriversal HCI** — many worlds, not one universal design ideal
* **Digital wellbeing** — attention, boundaries, self-regulation
* **Human-AI interaction / responsible AI** — agency, trust, opacity, accountability

## 6. Personal research lens

A useful bridge:

> Technology may not simply “make life fast.” It may amplify the user’s internal rhythm.

From a psychiatric or wellbeing perspective:

* racing thoughts can make tech feel faster
* anxiety can make notifications feel urgent
* poor sleep can reduce friction tolerance
* hypomanic energy can make digital environments more sticky
* calm or slow design may help when internal rhythm is vulnerable

Possible thesis lens:

> Technology as a rhythm amplifier.

This connects mental wellbeing, technoaesthetics, slow technology, and interaction design.

---

## 7. Working definition

**Counter-movement in HCI:**
A research direction that resists dominant technology values and proposes alternative values for designing human-technology relations.

**Technoaesthetics:**
The study/design of how technology feels, not only how it functions.

**Slow Technology:**
A counter-movement that designs technology for reflection, presence, and time, rather than speed and efficiency.
